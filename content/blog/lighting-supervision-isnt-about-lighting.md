Being good at lighting gets you into supervision. What keeps you there is something else entirely.

<!-- more -->

I've been a lighter for a long time. I know how to light a scene, how to read a comp, how to talk to a DOP reference. That knowledge doesn't go away when you step into a supervisory role — but pretty quickly you realise it's not the thing that's being tested anymore.

---

### What I thought the job was

> **Draft prompt:** What did you imagine supervision would look like before you were doing it? More technical decisions, more creative control, calling the shots on look? Paint the picture of what you assumed it would be.

---

### What it actually is

The craft is still there, but it's applied differently now.

> **Draft prompt:** What does a day actually look like? What percentage of your time is spent looking at pixels vs. talking to people? What kind of conversations are you having — with artists, with production, with other departments?

---

### The conversations that matter more than the craft

> **Draft prompt:** Think about a moment where your ability to communicate, manage expectations, or read a room made more difference than any technical decision you made. You don't have to name names — but what happened, and what did it teach you?

The hard truth is that the artists on your team are often just as good at lighting as you are. Possibly better in some areas. That's fine — that's the point. Your job becomes something else.

> **Draft prompt:** How do you think about your role relative to the artists on your team? What does good supervision feel like from the artist's side, in your view?

---

### What you have to be careful not to lose

Getting further from the work has trade-offs.

> **Draft prompt:** Is there anything you miss about just lighting shots? Do you worry about staying sharp technically? How do you keep a foot in the craft while running a department?

---

### What I'm still figuring out

> **Draft prompt:** Be honest — what part of the role still challenges you? What's the thing you haven't fully cracked yet? This is the section that makes the post feel real rather than like a list of lessons learned.

---

### For anyone moving into supervision

> **Draft prompt:** One or two things you'd tell a senior lighter who's eyeing the step up. Not a pep talk — something actually useful that you wish someone had said to you.
